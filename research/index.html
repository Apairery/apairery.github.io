Thesis

  1. How Margin Trading and Short Sale Reduce Stock Market Liquidity: Evidence from China. Undergraduate Thesis. 2019.
    Presented at the Fifth Conference of Theory of the Financial Business Cycles, Dec 2019, Yunnan, China.
    Keywords: Margin Trading; Short Sale; Liquidity; Heterogeneous Beliefs
    AbstractLiquidity is an important quality measure of financial market. The margin trading and short sale initiated in China in 2010 is an important reform. Theoretically, the reform allows opinions of both optimists and pessimists (through leverage and short sale, respectively) to be incorporated into the stock prices, therefore it can enhance the market liquidity. This study trys to identify its causal effects by exploiting the quasi-natural experiment in China, using a Difference-in-Differences (DID) methodology. The paper finds that margin trading reduces liquidity, while short sale enhances liquidity. Overall, since margin trading is dominating short sale, the reform reduces market liquidity. The study suggests that margin trading and short sale need to be more balanced, in order to enhance the market liquidity.

  2. Examining the NYPD Arrests Dataset. Course project for ORIE 4741: Learning with Big Messy Data. Fall 2019. [Github]
    Our final report got 4.82/5 (one of the best two)!
    Keywords: Logistic Regression; SVM; Random Forest; Fairness
    AbstractIn New York City, the stop-question-and-frisk program is a NYC Police Department (NYPD) practice of temporarily detaining, questioning and searching civilians on the street. However, this program became the subject of a racial profiling controversy. The executive director of the New York Civil Liberties Union once pointed out no meaningful progress was made in reducing racial disparities in the program. In this report, we fitted a logistic regression classifier and a kernel SVM classifier to predict which types of suspects would be arrested. F 1 scores of the two classifiers are 0.825 and 0.861 and AUC values are 0.915 and 0.852 respectively. We found that carrying contrabands and weapons is a sign of being arrested. Besides, the arrestment decision also depends on time, location, and the economic situation. Furthermore, based on demographic parity and equalized odds, we found the unfairness in the decision of arrestment over sex and race, which supports the existence of disparities.
